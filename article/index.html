    Abstract¶

    Whilst the nascent discipline known as Research Software Engineering, and more generally the need for professional software management and data stewardship in academic research, is becoming increasingly recognised, adoption can be patchy and the message can be slow to get though. By adopting the pricinciples of RSE, researchers can benefit by...

    We argue that firstly, Research Software Engineering should be considered an academic discipline in its own right. If implemented correctly, it can greatly enhance reproducibility of research, minimise duplicated effort, and - by open-sourcing your work for your peers - avoid potentially costly mistakes and retractions. The technologies used to achieve this are constantly changing, and new technologies are always emerging - investigating their applicability/utility to this discipline is a research field in it's own right. More importantly, we argue that the prime motivation of Research Software Engineering should be to educate and inspire researchers to adopt good practices, and provide tools that enable them to do so with the minimum of effort, rather than simply doing the work for them.

    Also availability...

    One major benefit of good practice is that it enables researchers to revisit software they may not have touched in years, implemented in a framework that means they are safe in the knowledge that it still works correctly - how many COVID-19 researchers who last year had to very quickly dust off their old epidemiological models were in a position to hit the ground running?

    You can easily see how this enhanced agility researchers gain in adopting these practices could translate directly to human lives saved.

    Method¶

    Takeup is patchy, perhaps more so in the social rather than the hard sciences. Perhaps one reason for this is the media via which the message is offen communicated - publishing in hard science or computer science journals is to some extent preaching to the converted e.g. https://arxiv.org/pdf/2002.01035.pdf. The message needs to be broadcast more widely across all disciplines.

    But it's more effort?¶

    Yes, it might well be for you, and in the short term. But a well-engineered, tested and documented software package, or a well managed, versioned dataset, can be used by anyone and could save other researchers huge amounts of time. The benefit to the research community as a whole is enormous.

    While you may need to budget for extra development time and resources in your grant, your software becomes a research output in itself. This is increasingly recognised(?). Other researchers can then leverage your work and progress further than they would have been able to otherwise. Likewise, and in turn, you will benefit from other researchers providing software for you to use.

    Conclusion¶

    The discipline of RSE started in the UK and is being taken up globally, but patchy.

    RSE should not be seen as a service provided to reserachers, or good software data stewardship practices seen as tangential to the real research effort. Software is integral to the research effort and should be seen as a discipline in it own right, even an academic one.
